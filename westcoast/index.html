It's time to say goodbye to the city of angels and hit the road, next time we will come back here only at the very end of the trip and only at the airport. But I would like to tell you about one more thing - the roads of Los Angeles. In fact, almost the entire city is an endless residential one-story blocks, stretching for tens of kilometers. But you get used to it very quickly and realize that the entire road system is very well thought out.

Master plan of Metropolitan Los Angeles freeways, 1947

So the roads in the United States are great, especially their highway system. There are four main types of roads in the United States highway system. Each highway of any type has its own number and its own road sign with a unique design that makes it easy and quick to identify the road.

  1. Interstates - the backbone of the U.S. highway network. The highways that carry millions of people across the country. Designed and built to specific standards for the most efficient and safe high-speed travel. Funded by the state, and maintained and repaired by the authorities of the state through which a section of road passes. They are globally numbered within the country, and on the road they are usually identified by a number on a blue and red shield.
  2. U.S. highways - an older system of state highways, but since the advent of interstates, the role of this type of road has declined to medium-range travel, mostly within a single state. Can be built to high safety standards, or be a regular 2-lane road without a divider. Repair and maintenance of such roads is shared between state and local authorities. The numbering is similar to the interstate, and they are identified by a number on a white board.
  3. State highways - as the name shows, roads within the same state. They are also built to different standards and their quality varies significantly. These roads are fully managed by the state authorities. They have their own intra-state numbering and the coolest badges (each state has its own design).
  4. County highways - local roads in some states. They can be either major roads or unpaved roads leading to remote areas. The provision of such roads is entirely the responsibility of the local government. Signs, like the previous type of road, also have their own design and numbering.

In fact, there are other types of roads in some states, but not many and for the most part they are local roads. But we were waiting for a road number 1...

California 1

California State Route 1, SR1 or Pacific Coast Highway, is one of the most beautiful roads in the United States, running along most of the California coast, next to one of the most scenic coastlines in the world.

Pacific Coast Highway road sign

The road is 1,056 km long and begins near Dana Point (a small town southeast of LA). It is almost always along the coast, passing around numerous bays, going up into the mountains and back down to the water. It goes through two major metropolitan areas - Los Angeles and San Francisco, sometimes matching the 101 freeway on its way, and ending in the settlement of Leggett (where only 122 people live) in northern California. The road was not built all at once, but gradually in pieces since 1934 and was more of a collection of different roads until 1964, when it was given the number SR 1.

We decided not to do a detour to Dana Point, but just drove a couple of kilometers from the hotel to the ocean and took Highway 1 there. The next thing we had to do was to drive 1,000 kilometers on the one road.

We left Los Angeles early in the morning to avoid the traffic, but somehow we jammed anyway. The streets of the city, the suburbs, small satellite towns, and cottage communities-it would take more than a dozen miles to get out of civilization. But the cabins and parking lots became much rarer, and all that was left was the road, the sandy mountains on one side, the ocean on the other, and the occasional car with surfers along the curb. The atmosphere of a road trip was beginning to emerge.

I will make a small digression. If you are planning a road trip in the USA, it is useful to know at least the basic differences between American and European traffic rules and the basic terms that you will encounter on the road. Below I've gathered some basic terms and concepts about U.S. roads.

  • Highway - in some contries this term is mistakenly used for an expressway or a freeway, but in fact the highway is just a road, any road even unpaved.
  • Divided / undivided highway - these terms refer to roads with or without a physical separator between oncoming traffic. There are even special signs for this.
  • Freeway - expressway. A highway built to special standards (for example, such highways do not intersect with other roads on the same level, and there are entrances and exits to such highways only in certain places) and designed for high-speed travel. All interstates in the United States are in this category.
  • Backroad - secondary road, for example, in the countryside.
  • One-way / two-way street
  • Turnpike - toll road.
  • Overpass - elevated road.
  • Underpass - road passing under an overpass, bridge or other road.
  • Intersection / crossroad
  • Roundabout - circular intersection or junction.
  • PED XING - pedestrian crosswalk.
  • Dead end - no exit road.
  • No outlet - just like a dead end, but is not used for a single road, but for a network of roads from which there is only one entrance or exit.
  • Lane
  • Line
  • Shoulder - the side of the road.
  • Sidewalk
  • Carpool lane - a special lane in which only cars with several people (at least 2, as a rule) or buses have the right to move.
  • Turnout - a special right-hand lane on narrow two-lane roads (usually in the mountains or hilly terrain) used by slow vehicles so that others can pass them.
  • Speed limit
  • Towing vehicle - car with a trailer.
  • Traffic light / signal
  • Stop all-way - special intersections with Stop signs in all directions. Very common in California. When you approach such an intersection, you must stop and continue only if there is no interference. If you are approaching more than one car, the first car to pass will be the first one to pass.
  • U-turn - turn around.
  • Yield - give way to other traffic.
  • Passing
  • Merging
  • Tailgating - not keeping the distance by the car moving behind, close to the car in front. It is considered a serious violation and you may be fined for it.
  • Traffic jam
  • Hazard

San Jose & San Francisco

From the hills we descend into one of the suburbs of San Jose. Although almost the whole city looks like a suburb - blocks of small one-story houses with small streets and a freeway a couple of kilometers away, cutting off all this quiet idyll from another one just like the previous one.

The contrast with other communities we've seen before is obvious. Here are some of the most expensive neighborhoods in the U.S., next door to Silicon Valley, the world center of IT technology. Perfect lawns, trees, roads, expensive cars and luxury homes.

Through the city's freeway system we reach our friend who lives in north San Jose. We spend the next few days here, making occasional trips into the neighborhood and into San Francisco.

San Jose
Approaching San Francisco

Although San Francisco is close by (especially if you look at the map), it's at least an hour one-way. It could be more, depending on traffic. And in fact, it is San Francisco that is the only full-fledged city in the area, since downtown San Jose is really very small and compact.

The city itself is sometimes like New York, sometimes like Los Angeles. But even if it's smaller than those two cities, it definitely has a character of its own.

SF Downtown

Much of the credit for this goes to the terrain. The city stands on many hills. That's why the streets here go down and up, looking from afar like completely steep walls, with houses somehow attached to them.

Downtown is pure New York City. Huge skyscrapers, people in suits and homeless with posters painted on cardboards.

And of course, the most important symbol of the city - the Golden Gate Bridge. A huge (2.7 km long) suspension bridge of red-brick color, in fact, the most famous bridge in the world. It was built in 1937 and linked two banks of the bay, almost 30 years remained the largest bridge in the world.

When we approached it, a very cold and strong wind was blowing from the ocean. And looking at the crowns of the trees bent toward the coast, it's a regular thing here. What's interesting is that literally a few kilometers inland and the climate changes, it becomes much warmer.

Golden Gate Bridge

Long way back

After standing in the morning traffic, we leave town and turn back to the coast on Highway 101.

The coast here is heavily riddled with limans and marshes, so the road heavily winds, revealing sometimes quite taiga scenery.

We are approaching the Washington-Oregon border on the Columbia River. The river here forms a very wide delta before flowing into the ocean; we cross it over a very long (about 6 km) bridge and arrive in the town of Astoria, Oregon.

Astoria-Megler Bridge

Astoria is an ordinary little west coast town in the United States, it wouldn't stand out against hundreds of other such towns, but someone in 1926 decided to build a Roman column on a local hill with an observation deck on top.

From the top you can get a good view of the surroundings: the city itself, the river with the bridge, the whole coast and the ocean.

Columbia River Delta

After driving a little further, we reach a place called Cannon Beach.

Cannon Beach

The rocky forest coast gives a way here to a wide sandy beach covered with reeds, and a huge lonely rock peeking out a few tens of meters from the shore.

We are lucky to catch the tide, so the beach looks even wider and the waves seem to be frozen.

Haystack Rock

The daylight hours are already quite short and the sun is getting closer and closer to the horizon. We get in the car and drive on, with a strong desire to drive all over Oregon for the rest of the day.

Twilight, pine forests, and the occasional lumber trucks along the way.

We go back to Interstate 5, put the car on a cruise and drive like that until night.

We're staying for the night in some little town near the California border, the name of which I don't remember anymore.

Early in the morning, after breakfast, we're out on the interstate again. I must say that driving on such interstates is not as interesting as on small roads, but it saves a lot of time and allows you to get where you need to go very fast.

It's California again, the amazing thing is that as soon as we crossed the state line, the previously gloomy sky immediately became perfectly clear. It's always sunny in California.

But the weather is actually deceptive and as soon as you get out of the car you immediately feel that it's actually quite chilly outside.

A tall mountain appears on the horizon, it's Mount Shasta and it's actually a dormant volcano.

It would be worth telling here that a lot of the peaks of the Cascade Mountains (a range running along the entire west coast of North America) are actually volcanoes and are part of the so-called Pacific Volcanic Ring of Fire, a system of volcanoes along the entire border of the lithospheric plate beneath the Pacific Ocean.

Yes, all these mountains from the Seattle and Portland landscapes (Mount Rainier and Mount Hood) are dormant volcanoes that will definitely erupt in the future. How close, only the U.S. Geological Survey, which monitors the seismic activity of these peaks, can tell.

Mount Shasta

We also stop at a dam with the same name, Shasta Dam, located slightly off the road. The dam was built in 1945 and is used for generating electricity, storing fresh water, and controlling floods. And anyone can come and walk on it, the entrance is free.

Shasta Dam

By evening we reach Sacramento, the state capital, but not the largest city.

The next morning we continue. As we approach the huge Bay Area agglomeration, the landscape outside the window becomes more and more urbanized, and there are more and more cars on the roads.

After San Jose, we turn off and go inland toward the Sierra Nevada Mountains.

We want to go to Sequoia National Park. It's November and there may already be snow in the mountains, so we call the park beforehand and see if we can drive through without chains.

Sequoia National Park

The road turns into a serpentine and begins to climb steeply.

As you climb, nature changes, the trees get taller and the first sequoias appear.

We reach a place called the Giant Forest. Here grows the largest (by volume, not height) tree on the planet, the Giant Sequoia General Sherman. The tree is about 2300-2700 years old and grows surrounded by similar giants.

General Sherman

Right on the parking lot, right next to the car we meet a wild deer!

Wild Deer

And on the way we catch the most beautiful flaming sunset.

Sierra Nevada

The car is returned, the bags are packed and we are sitting in the lounge waiting for our return flight.

3 states, 6 major cities and dozens of smaller ones, 12 motels, 5,500 km of roads, 450 liters of gasoline and so much memories. I'm starting to sort all the pictures I made and beginning to write this report.
